# FOAM — Social Media Brand Guide

> The brand shows up the same way every time. Not rigid. Consistent. There's a difference.

---

## Platform Overview

| Platform | Primary Role | Primary Audience | Content Priority |
|----------|-------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Instagram | Brand identity + community | Detailers, shop operators + car culture | Visual-first, craft-forward |
| TikTok | Growth + organic reach | Mobile detailers (younger ops), shop owners + customers | Education + entertainment |
| Facebook | Community + local reach | All operator types + local customers | Community + ads |
| Meta Ads | Paid acquisition | All segments, geo-targeted | Conversion-focused |

## 01 — Visual Templates

Every template is built on the same foundation: **dark background, cobalt accent, Outfit typeface, clean composition**. The visual system doesn't change between templates — only the purpose does.

---

### Template 1: The Statement Post
**Used for:** Bold brand messages, product truths, industry observations. High-impact, low-information.

**What it looks like:**
Near-black background (#0D0D0D). Single headline in Outfit 800 weight, 40-48px, centered or left-aligned with generous breathing room. The text is the hero. No photography. No clutter. Optional: one thin cobalt rule (2px) above or below the headline as a graphic element. The FOAM wordmark sits small in the bottom right corner — present but not competing.

**Example text treatments:**
- "Clean cars. Real money."
- "Stop managing your business from your DMs."
- "The tools you needed. Finally built for you."
- "The whiteboard had a good run. It's done now."

**Aspect ratios:** 1:1 for feed, 9:16 for Stories/Reels cover frame

---

### Template 2: The Craft Post
**Used for:** Before/after content, job showcases, detailing process photography — mobile and shop work equally. This is where the work speaks.

**What it looks like:**
Full-bleed photography — either a single strong image or a left/right split for before/after. If split: a thin 3px cobalt dividing line separates the panels, with small "BEFORE" and "AFTER" labels in Outfit 600 uppercase at 11px, +0.08em letter-spacing, anchored to the bottom of each panel. The FOAM mark appears as a semi-transparent watermark in one corner — enough to identify the brand, not enough to compete with the photo.

For single images: photography takes 85% of the frame. A dark gradient footer (transparent to #0D0D0D over 120px) anchors the bottom, where a short caption line in Outfit 500 at 14px sits. Example: "Full interior + exterior. Atlanta." Short. Located. Real.

**Location tags for craft posts:** Always include the Atlanta neighborhood or city. For shop-based work, the shop name or area is a trust signal. "Full detail. Buckhead shop." works as well as "Full detail. Mobile. Midtown."

**Aspect ratios:** 1:1 for feed, 4:5 for portrait feed, 9:16 for Stories

---

### Template 3: The Proof Post
**Used for:** Reviews, milestone numbers, detailer and shop stats, platform metrics. Social proof with visual punch.

**What it looks like:**
Dark card (#161616) with a subtle cobalt border (1px, 40% opacity) on the left edge only — like a highlighted citation. The review text or stat sits in Inter 400 at 16px in off-white (#F5F5F5). Attribution sits below in Outfit 600 at 12px in cobalt (#3B82F6) — the operator's name, shop name, or stat source. A five-star row uses the amber (#F59E0B) star treatment from the design system.

For milestone/number posts: the number itself goes enormous — Outfit 800, 64-80px, in cobalt. Supporting context in Inter 400 at 14px in secondary text below it. "Bays filled this month across Atlanta." or "Details completed on FOAM this month." Clean. Let the number do the work.

**Aspect ratios:** 1:1 for feed, 9:16 for Stories

---

### Template 4: The Education Post
**Used for:** Tips, how-tos, business advice for mobile detailers and shop operators. Carousel format primarily. This is the content that builds the audience.

**What it looks like:**
Carousel slides with a consistent header bar — cobalt strip (48px tall) at the top of every slide, containing the episode/tip number in Outfit 700 at 13px uppercase in white ("TIP 03" / "STEP 02") and a short topic label. The rest of the slide is dark (#0D0D0D or #161616) with content. First slide is a hook statement in Outfit 700 at 28-32px. Subsequent slides use Inter 400 at 16px for body with Outfit 600 at 18px subheads. Final slide always ends with the FOAM wordmark centered and a single CTA.

**Aspect ratios:** 1:1 carousel

---

### Template 5: The Culture Post
**Used for:** Community moments, operator spotlights (mobile and shop), founder content, behind-the-scenes. The most human content in the feed.

**What it looks like:**
Most flexible template. The photography and authenticity are the design here — no heavy graphic treatment. Minimal text overlays. The FOAM mark appears small. The person, the car, the bay, or the moment is the entire point. If text appears, it's a single short line in Outfit 700, bottom-anchored on a dark gradient, never covering faces.

For operator spotlights: a name treatment card overlays the bottom third. Cobalt first name in Outfit 800 at 28px. City in Outfit 400 at 14px in secondary text below. Business name — van operation or shop name — if applicable. Nothing else.

**Aspect ratios:** 4:5 for feed, 9:16 for Stories/Reels

---

### Template 6: The Ad Unit
**Used for:** Paid acquisition on Meta/Instagram. Designed to stop the scroll and drive a specific action.

**What it looks like:**
Three variants depending on audience segment.

*Mobile detailer acquisition ad:* Statement template with a sharper hook. "Still managing your bookings through Instagram?" in Outfit 700. Below it, three short proof points in Inter 500 at 14px with cobalt checkmark icons. CTA button in cobalt: "Get FOAM Free" or "Join Free." Dark background throughout.

*Shop operator acquisition ad:* Statement template with a different hook — aimed at the bay, not the van. "Still running your shop on a whiteboard?" in Outfit 700. Below it, three proof points specific to shop operations: "Bay management built in. Walk-in flow handled. Customer history that doesn't disappear." Same cobalt checkmarks. Same CTA: "Get FOAM Free." The visual distinction from the mobile ad matters — show a shop environment, not a van.

*Customer acquisition ad:* Craft template leading. Strong before/after or clean car image. Minimal text. Headline: "The best detailers in [City] — mobile or shop — bookable in 2 minutes." CTA: "Book Now." No clutter. The image converts — not the copy.

**Aspect ratios:** 1:1 and 4:5 for feed ads, 9:16 for Stories/Reels ads

## 02 — Caption Style

### Tone
Direct. Energetic. Grounded in the industry — mobile and fixed alike. Reads like a confident person who has done this work at every scale, not a social media manager who studied it. The brand voice from BRAND_DNA.md applies here without exception.

### Length by Platform and Content Type

| Platform | Statement/Craft | Education | Community |
|----------|----------------|-----------|-----------|
| Instagram | 1-3 lines + hashtags | 3-5 lines + hashtags | 2-4 lines, no hashtag wall |
| TikTok | 1-2 lines max | 1-2 lines, let video carry | 1-2 lines |
| Facebook | 2-4 lines, slightly more context | 4-6 lines | 3-5 lines, invite conversation |

**The rule:** If you need more than 3 lines on Instagram to say it, the visual isn't working hard enough. The image or video should carry the weight. The caption finishes the thought.

### Caption Structure

**Opening line** — This is the only line most people read. It needs to earn the rest. No preamble. No "We're excited to share." Start mid-thought.

Strong opening patterns:
- Declarative statement: "This is what running a real detailing business looks like."
- Direct address: "Detailers — your next customer just searched for you."
- The number: "47 cars. One week. One guy."
- The contrast: "Before: voicemail. After: two-minute booking."
- Shop-specific: "3 bays. Tuesday morning. All booked. That's what FOAM does."

**Body** — Only if the opening earns it. Add context, story, or specific detail. No filler. No fluff.

**Close** — Either a CTA ("Link in bio to get started"), a question that invites genuine response ("What's the one thing that would make your day run cleaner?"), or nothing. Silence is better than a weak close.

---

### Signature Phrases

These are FOAM's verbal fingerprints. Use them consistently enough that the audience starts to recognize them as distinctly FOAM.

| Phrase | Context |
|--------|---------|
| "Clean business. Clean car." | Universal — works for all operator types and customers |
| "That's what it's about." | After a milestone, a 5-star review, a before/after |
| "Built for the work." | When talking about the platform features |
| "Van or bay. FOAM handles both." | When speaking to the dual operator audience |
| "The whiteboard days are over." | Shop operator-specific — sharp contrast |
| "Your payout. Your numbers. Your call." | Business empowerment — mobile and shop |

---

### What to Never Write

- "We're excited to announce..."
- "Our team has been working hard to..."
- "In today's digital landscape..."
- "Seamlessly" or "robust" or "streamlined experience"
- Any caption that could have been written by a generic SaaS company

## 03 — Content Categories

Six content categories. Every post belongs to exactly one. Know which one before writing the first word.

---

### Category 1: The Work
**What it is:** Craft content. Before/after photography, job showcases, finished vehicles. This is the product of what FOAM operators do. Show it with respect — whether it came off a mobile van or out of a shop bay.
**Platform priority:** Instagram (primary), TikTok (video format)
**Visual template:** Craft Post (Template 2)
**Frequency:** 2x per week
**Feel:** Let the work speak. Minimal copy. Maximum visual. This content does not need to explain itself.
**Caption style:** Short. Specific. Located. "Full ceramic. Decatur shop. 4 hours. Worth every minute."
**What this content proves:** FOAM operators do exceptional work. Both the mobile ones and the shop ones.

---

### Category 2: The Business Reality
**What it is:** Operator-focused content about running a real detailing business — mobile or fixed — tips, truths, industry observations, the unglamorous side of building something.
**Platform priority:** TikTok (primary for reach), Instagram Carousels, Facebook
**Visual template:** Education Post (Template 4) for carousels, Statement Post (Template 1) for single frames
**Frequency:** 2x per week
**Feel:** Operator-to-operator. Honest. No sugarcoating. This content positions FOAM as the brand that actually understands detailing operations at every level.
**Caption style:** Direct address. Specific, not general. Mobile or shop context depending on the post.
**Example topics — mobile:**
- "3 things that are quietly killing your recurring revenue"
- "How to price a full detail so you're actually making money"
- "The difference between a $300 week and a $3,000 week isn't skill"

**Example topics — shop:**
- "How to fill dead bay time without discounting your services"
- "The walk-in customer you lose every week and don't know about"
- "Why your repeat customers stopped coming back"

**What this content proves:** FOAM knows the business — van and bay. It's not a tech company cosplaying as an industry insider.

---

### Category 3: Social Proof
**What it is:** Reviews, ratings, operator milestones, platform stats, customer testimonials.
**Platform priority:** Instagram, Facebook, Meta Ads
**Visual template:** Proof Post (Template 3)
**Frequency:** 1x per week
**Feel:** Confident but not boastful. Let the numbers and words from real people do the talking.
**Caption style:** Minimal. One line of context, then silence. "47 five-star reviews. First 30 days. Atlanta." or "3 bays. Fully booked. Every Saturday. One shop. FOAM."
**What this content proves:** FOAM works. Real operators. Real customers. Real results — on both sides of the business.

---

### Category 4: Community and Culture
**What it is:** Operator spotlights (mobile detailers and shop owners), founder content, behind-the-scenes of building FOAM, Atlanta moments, industry culture, car culture.
**Platform priority:** Instagram (grid and Stories), TikTok, Facebook
**Visual template:** Culture Post (Template 5)
**Frequency:** 1x per week
**Feel:** Human. Warm. Unpolished in the right ways. Introduce real operators — the van guy who has been detailing since 18, the shop owner who turned one bay into four.
**Caption style:** More words allowed here. Tell a real story. Introduce a real person. Share something that actually happened.
**Founder content angle:** A first-generation American who ran a van and three shops in Atlanta and built the software this industry needed. That story is worth telling — more than once, across multiple formats.
**What this content proves:** There are real people behind this brand. The founder story matters. The operator community — in all its formats — is the entire point.

---

### Category 5: Product Education
**What it is:** Feature reveals, how-to content, platform walkthroughs, tips for using FOAM.
**Platform priority:** TikTok (screen recordings + voiceover), Instagram Carousels, Facebook
**Visual template:** Education Post (Template 4) for static, screen-capture for video
**Frequency:** 1x per week
**Feel:** Practical. Fast. Show the value, don't just claim it. Separate walkthroughs for mobile operator features and shop operator features when relevant — they have different workflows and different audiences.
**Caption style:** Specific. Outcome-first. "This is how FOAM shop operators manage bays, walk-ins, and booked appointments from one screen." Then show it.
**What this content proves:** The product is real, it works, and it's designed for people who actually do this work — on the road and in the shop.

---

### Category 6: Brand Statements
**What it is:** Pure brand voice. Bold copy. The brand's worldview. These posts don't promote a feature or showcase a job — they state what FOAM believes.
**Platform priority:** Instagram (high save/share rate for this format), Meta Ads as creative
**Visual template:** Statement Post (Template 1)
**Frequency:** 1x per week or less — these are the rarest posts and should feel like they mean something
**Feel:** Sharp. Ownable. The kind of thing someone screenshots and sends to a detailer or shop owner friend.
**Caption style:** 1-2 lines maximum. Sometimes nothing but the image.
**Example treatments:**
- "Good enough was never the standard. It was just the only option."
- "The best detailers in your city don't need a bigger platform. They need a better one."
- "Van or bay. You deserve the same tools."
- "Run clean."
**What this content proves:** FOAM has a point of view. It's not just a platform — it's a brand that believes something about this entire industry.

## 04 — Brand Elements in Every Post

These five elements must appear in every piece of FOAM content regardless of format, platform, or content category. Non-negotiable.

### 1. The FOAM Mark
Every post carries the FOAM wordmark or logo mark — full wordmark on most formats, mark-only on photography-heavy content where the wordmark competes. Placement: bottom right on static images, first and last frame on video. Minimum size: readable without zooming on a phone screen.

### 2. The Color Signature
Near-black backgrounds on graphic content. Cobalt blue (#2563EB / #3B82F6 on dark) as the only accent color. This is what makes FOAM instantly recognizable in a scroll even before the eye processes the logo. The color palette must be consistent across every template, every platform.

### 3. The Typeface
Outfit for any display text in branded graphics. No exceptions. If a designer, contractor, or content creator submits something set in a different typeface, it goes back for revision. This is a non-negotiable visual identifier.

### 4. The Voice Standard
The Brand DNA voice applies to every caption, every comment, every response to a DM shown publicly. Straight. Industry-fluent. Energetic but earned. Respectful of operators. If a caption sounds like it came from a generic SaaS company or a car wash chain — or like it only understands one side of this industry — it doesn't ship.

### 5. Location Identity (Launch Phase)
During the Atlanta launch period, every post that references a specific job, operator, or booking includes Atlanta or a specific Atlanta neighborhood. This anchors the brand to its market, builds local recognition, and signals that FOAM is real and active — not a ghost brand promising a future app.

## 05 — The Five Things FOAM Never Does

### 1. Never Posts Generic Car Content
A sunset photo of a Ferrari with a vague caption about passion. A stock photo of someone washing a car. Inspirational car quotes with no connection to the brand or the industry. This kills the operator credibility that FOAM has built. Every car, every job, every image should be connected to a real operator — mobile or shop — doing real work. If it could have been posted by a car wash chain, it doesn't belong here.

### 2. Never Uses Corporate Enthusiasm
Exclamation points on product announcements. "We're SO excited to share!!!" "HUGE news, fam!!!" This is the fastest way to sound like every startup social account that nobody follows. FOAM's confidence is quiet. The brand knows what it is. It doesn't need to tell you how excited it is. It just shows you what it does.

### 3. Never Punches at Competitors by Name
FOAM doesn't mention Washos, Spiffy, Booksy, Vagaro, or Square by name on social. Not to compare. Not to criticize. Not to "position." The brand is too confident for that. The real enemy isn't a competitor — it's "good enough." That's the only thing FOAM is fighting publicly. Naming competitors makes FOAM look small. Ignoring them makes FOAM look like it already won.

### 4. Never Posts Without a Visual System
A screenshot from someone's phone with no brand treatment. A graphic made in a different color palette because "it was just a quick post." A template with the wrong typeface because someone was in a hurry. Every post that breaks the visual system is an un-branding event. The consistency IS the brand recognition. One off-brand post chips at the equity every on-brand post built.

### 5. Never Forgets the Shop Operator
A feed that only shows vans, routes, and mobile setups actively excludes half the operator audience FOAM serves. Shop owners need to see themselves in the content — bays, walk-ins, drop-offs, the reality of running a fixed location. Before any post goes out, ask: does this make a shop owner feel like FOAM was built for them too? If the answer is no, add the perspective or save it for a post that targets mobile operators explicitly. Know who the post is for. Speak to them directly.
